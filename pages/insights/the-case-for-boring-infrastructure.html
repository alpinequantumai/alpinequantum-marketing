The case for boring infrastructure.

Why we still reach for Postgres + Spring Boot + a single VPS for new internal apps — and what we'd reach for if we couldn't.

KK
Ketul Kumar
Co-Founder & CTO · Alpine Quantum
May 18, 2026 8 min read

The most common technical question we get from clients in their first few weeks of engagement is some variant of: "Should we use [trendy thing]?" The thing changes — last year it was Kubernetes, this year it's serverless, next year it'll be something else — but the question is the same.

Our default answer is "almost certainly not." The case for boring infrastructure is one of the most consistent themes in our engineering practice, and it's worth being explicit about why.

What "boring" actually means.

Boring infrastructure has three properties. First, it's been deployed in production by thousands of teams for at least a decade. Second, the failure modes are well-understood and documented. Third, the operational knowledge needed to run it is widely available — your team probably already has it, or can hire it without paying a premium.

Postgres is boring. So is Spring Boot, Linux, Nginx, and Redis. Postgres has been in production at meaningful scale since the mid-1990s. Whatever you encounter operating it, somebody has encountered before and written about it.

Why we still default to boring choices for internal tools.

Our internal employee portal — leave management, timesheets, project allocation, the works for our 10-person company — runs on Postgres on Neon, Spring Boot on Render, and a React app on Cloudflare Pages. Total infrastructure cost: $10/month.

We could have built this on Kubernetes. We could have used a serverless architecture. We could have adopted a fancy event-sourcing framework. We didn't, because the boring stack does everything our 10-user portal needs while costing us approximately zero ongoing operational attention.

The right question isn't "is this technology cool?" The right question is "how much of my future time will I spend operating it?"

The same logic applies to most client work. If your team is going to operate the system after we hand it over, the infrastructure choices need to match your team's actual capabilities — not aspirational capabilities you haven't built yet.

When we do reach for newer infrastructure.

Boring isn't a religion. Three patterns of work justify newer infrastructure choices:

  • Genuinely high scale — when your workload genuinely exceeds what boring tools handle gracefully, the newer architectures earn their complexity.
  • Genuinely real-time — sub-100ms response requirements push you toward newer streaming and edge architectures.
  • Genuinely multi-region — when you actually have users in three continents with strict latency SLAs, the multi-region patterns are warranted.

The key word in all three is "genuinely." Most projects don't actually have these requirements; they have aspirational versions of them, picked up from blog posts about Netflix architecture.

What boring infrastructure costs us.

There's a cost. Boring infrastructure is sometimes slightly slower to develop on initially — fewer modern conveniences, less impressive in demos, doesn't generate excitement on the team. Engineers who joined for the chance to use cutting-edge technology sometimes leave for places that promise to.

We've made peace with these costs because the alternative is worse. Teams that chase technology fashion end up rewriting their infrastructure every 18 months. Each rewrite is justified at the time and looks defensible in isolation. The cumulative cost — both financial and in lost productivity — is enormous.

The default position.

Our default position is therefore: start with the boring stack, and require explicit justification to deviate. Postgres until proven inadequate. Spring Boot or Django until proven inadequate. A single VPS until proven inadequate.

Most projects never reach the "proven inadequate" threshold. They just ship.
