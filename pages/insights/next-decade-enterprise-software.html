The next decade of enterprise software.

Three predictions about how enterprise software will be built, bought, and operated by 2035 — and what they mean for technology leaders today.

KP
[Founder Name]
Co-Founder & CEO · Alpine Quantum
April 14, 2026 12 min read

Predicting the next decade of enterprise software is a fool's errand. Five years ago, no serious analyst predicted that natural language interfaces would become the dominant pattern for so many software products. Three years ago, nobody was talking about "agents" the way we are now. The honest position is humility.

That said, after twenty years working in and around enterprise technology, three patterns feel directionally correct to me. I think enterprise software will look meaningfully different by 2035 in three specific ways.

1. The "buy a SaaS" default weakens significantly.

For the past 15 years, the default answer to "should we build it or buy it" has been "buy." The economics were overwhelming: specialized SaaS vendors had better products, faster innovation, lower total cost than internal teams could realistically build.

I think that default weakens substantially in the next decade. Not because SaaS becomes bad — but because the cost of building has dropped. Modern infrastructure (managed databases, container platforms, identity-as-a-service), combined with AI-assisted engineering, means a small team can build and operate what used to require a dedicated product organization.

The math now: a 50-person enterprise might spend $200K/year on a SaaS HRIS. The same team could now build and operate something equivalent for closer to $20K/year in infrastructure + the engineering time of the team they already have. The break-even point on build-vs-buy has shifted.

I don't think every enterprise builds everything. But I think we'll see meaningful re-evaluation of the SaaS portfolios that most enterprises currently maintain. The 10-year SaaS relationship with annual price increases becomes harder to justify when the alternative has become genuinely tractable.

The next decade isn't SaaS vs internal build. It's internal builds that look as polished as SaaS, costing 10x less to operate.

2. The "platform" becomes more important than the application.

In 2025, most enterprise software discussions are about specific applications: which ATS, which CRM, which BI tool. By 2035, I think the discussions will be more about the underlying platform: the data layer, the integration fabric, the identity model, the AI orchestration layer.

The applications will be relatively interchangeable — built on top of the platform, swappable when needed, often partially or wholly custom. The platform is where the durable enterprise investment will live.

This is already happening in mature enterprise data organizations. The conversation has shifted from "which BI tool" to "what's our lakehouse architecture and governance model." Applications come and go on top of stable platforms.

I think the same pattern propagates to other domains. CRM platforms (the data, the workflow engine, the integration layer) become more durable than CRM applications. ERP platforms become more durable than ERP modules.

3. The "agent" layer becomes the primary interface for most workflows.

The third prediction is the most uncertain — but if true, the most consequential. I think a meaningful share of enterprise software interactions in 2035 happen through conversational agent interfaces rather than form-based UIs.

"Update the CRM" becomes "tell the CRM agent what happened on the call." "Run the quarterly report" becomes "ask the analytics agent what changed last quarter." "Submit an expense" becomes "tell the expense agent what you spent."

This isn't replacing all UI. Forms still exist where forms are the right interface. Dashboards still exist where dashboards are the right interface. But for the long tail of "make this thing in the system do this thing for me" interactions, conversational interfaces become the default.

The implication: enterprise software vendors that don't have a competent agent layer will be at structural disadvantage. The agent layer becomes a primary purchasing criterion, alongside the underlying functionality.

What this means for technology leaders today.

If these predictions are even partially correct, three practical implications for technology leaders right now:

1. Invest in platform thinking, not application thinking

Spend disproportionately on the durable platform investments: data architecture, identity, integration patterns, governance. These compound. Specific applications come and go.

2. Re-evaluate the build-vs-buy threshold

Many decisions that were correct as "buy" in 2020 deserve re-examination in 2026. Not all of them flip — but some do. The framework I'd recommend: explicitly model the cost of building plus operating, on a realistic 5-year horizon, factoring in your team's actual current capabilities (including AI assistance). The math has moved.

3. Build agent literacy now, not later

Even if your business doesn't have an immediate agent use case, your team should be developing the muscles to design, deploy, and operate agent systems. The capability takes time to build. By the time agent interfaces are commonplace, the teams that have practiced will have a meaningful advantage.

What I'm not predicting.

A few popular predictions I'm not making:

I'm not predicting AGI replaces software engineers. Engineering work shifts; engineers remain valuable. The skills that matter change; the need for skilled humans doesn't go away.

I'm not predicting the death of SaaS. SaaS as a category continues to grow, particularly in domains where compliance, scale, or specialization make building genuinely uneconomic. But the share of enterprise spending on traditional SaaS may peak.

I'm not predicting any specific company wins. The competitive dynamics of the next decade will be fascinating. I don't have a useful prediction about which vendors capture which markets.

The takeaway.

The next decade of enterprise software is more uncertain than usual. The fundamental cost structure of building software has shifted. The interface conventions are shifting. The vendor relationships are due for re-evaluation.

Technology leaders who lean into this — investing in platform thinking, re-examining build-vs-buy, building agent literacy — will be in a much better position than those waiting for clarity. The clarity isn't coming; the work needs to start now.
