Hiring senior engineers in 2026: what's changed.

What we look for, how we interview, and how the rise of AI coding tools is changing what "senior" actually means in software engineering.

PP
Pankaj P Patel
Co-Founder & CEO · Alpine Quantum
April 21, 2026 7 min read

We've made nine senior engineering hires in the past 18 months. The candidates we're now interviewing look different from candidates we were interviewing in 2023. So does our process. So does what we mean by "senior."

This post is about what's changed — and what hasn't.

What's actually changed.

The most visible change: every candidate now uses AI coding assistants. Five years ago we'd ask candidates to write code on a whiteboard, then a few years later in a shared editor, and now in an editor where they're explicitly allowed (and often expected) to use Copilot, Cursor, or Claude.

The interesting consequence is that the bar for "can you write code that works" has effectively collapsed. Any reasonable engineer with AI assistance can produce working code for most interview problems. So the signal we're actually looking for has shifted to higher levels of the engineering stack.

What "senior" now means.

We've recalibrated our definition of senior engineer to focus on three competencies that AI assistance doesn't replicate:

1. Judgment under ambiguity

Given a vague problem statement (a real one, the kind clients actually give us), can the candidate identify the right questions, scope the work, push back on assumptions, and make defensible architectural decisions?

This is the hardest thing to test in an interview and the most predictive thing for senior performance. We test it explicitly — usually by presenting a real (sanitized) client problem and walking through how the candidate would approach it.

2. Quality through review

Can the candidate look at a piece of code (often AI-generated) and identify what's wrong with it? Code review skill — the ability to read code critically, find bugs, suggest improvements, and articulate the trade-offs — has become more valuable as more code is generated rather than written.

We test this directly: we give candidates a chunk of AI-generated code with subtle problems, and ask them to review it as if a junior engineer wrote it.

In 2026, writing code is increasingly commoditized. Reviewing, scoping, and operating systems is where senior engineers earn their seniority.

3. Operational thinking

Can the candidate think about what happens after the code is deployed? Observability, failure modes, performance under load, debugging in production, on-call experience — these are the muscles that distinguish engineers who can ship things from engineers who can ship things and keep them running.

We probe this through war stories. "Tell me about a production incident you led the response on" gives us more signal than any technical problem.

What hasn't changed.

Despite the changes, the fundamentals of being a senior engineer haven't shifted:

  • Curiosity about how systems actually work. The willingness to dig into a black box rather than treat it as opaque.
  • Communication skill. The ability to explain technical concepts clearly to both engineers and non-engineers. AI doesn't substitute for this; it amplifies the gap between engineers who have it and those who don't.
  • Working memory for systems. The ability to hold the relevant parts of a complex system in your head, see how a change propagates, and reason about second-order effects.
  • Empathy for users. Senior engineers who don't think about the humans using their software ship worse software.

Our process, concretely.

Our hiring process for senior engineers is intentionally short:

  1. Intro call (30 min) — we both decide if there's mutual interest
  2. Technical conversation (90 min) — walking through a real problem from our work, with no whiteboarding
  3. Optional take-home (3-4 hours, paid) — only if the technical conversation left questions
  4. Founder conversation — final compatibility discussion

We've deliberately removed leetcode-style coding interviews. They were never predictive of senior performance, and they're particularly uninformative now. We've also removed system design as a separate round — system design is woven through the technical conversation.

Things we look for that other firms might miss.

A few specific things that have become more important to us:

Comfort being wrong publicly. Senior engineers who can't update their mental model when shown new information are dangerous. We push on this in interviews — propose alternatives, push back gently — and watch how the candidate responds.

Stories about saying no. Engineers who have never told stakeholders "no" are usually under-experienced or over-deferential. The ability to push back on requirements, scope, or design decisions — constructively — is a senior trait.

Self-direction. Can the candidate articulate what they want to be working on in 12 months? In 5 years? The candidates with no answer aren't bad engineers — they're just not yet senior in the way we mean it.

The takeaway.

Hiring senior engineers in 2026 is both harder (because the surface-level signals are less reliable) and easier (because the signals that do matter are more legible than ever). The teams that figure this out will outperform the teams that don't.
